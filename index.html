Why 180?

The 180 Program works to keep youth out of the juvenile justice system, bringing community leaders and prosecutors together to break the school-to-prison pipeline. King County Prosecuting Attorney Dan Satterberg asked Doug Wheeler, a community leader, for help to reach youth in a new way. Dan recognized that the criminal justice system is not the most effective tool in addressing complex social issues including getting at the heart of why juveniles often make poor choices. Dan recognized the answer would require a new messenger that is not part of the system.

So Doug Wheeler convened a team of community leaders to design a program with a new message to the youth that says, 'We care about you and we believe in you. We know you can do better and we will give you a path to make better choices.' The result of that partnership is the 180 Program, a pre-filing juvenile diversion program designed to disrupt the pattern of the criminal justice system that disproportionately punishes black and brown young men, which too often leads them to long-term involvement in the criminal justice system.

Of the nearly 2,000 youth who were charged and directed to King County's juvenile court system in 2014, three quarters of those were youth of color. By helping youth of color and other youth throughout King County, the 180 Program intervenes at a critical point to remove barriers for these youth to grow, strengthens communities, and saves over $500,000 a year in tax dollars which would otherwise be spent on criminal prosecution, court costs and juvenile incarceration. To date, this partnership has diverted over 1,400 youth away from the juvenile justice system.

Our Work

The 180 Program consists of two elements: 1) the half-day 180 Workshop and 2) Beyond 180 follow-up aftercare. Participating youth avoid criminal prosecution by completing the 180 Workshop. The Workshop uses an innovative and evidence-based approach to improving the culture of youth engagement by engaging inspiring representatives from the communities where youth live, many of whom experienced similar struggles as young people. Through a combination of large-group presentations, engaging ice-breaker activities, and small-group personal discussions, workshop participants learn to make better choices.

Beyond 180 provides follow-up after the Workshop to help youth apply what they learned and supports their new 180 change. Youth voluntarily sign-up to be paired with a Community Ambassador who will walk hand-in-hand with them to access the services the need and build a personal relationship with them to encourage them to continue on a positive path.

Studies conducted by the University of Washington's Public Behavioral Health and Justice Policy division and King County's Office of Performance, Strategy and Budget show that the 180 Program is more effective than an established comparison program at reducing recidivism and addressing disproportionality.

The 180 Program is a program of the Community Leaders Round Table of Seattle (CLRS), a 501(c)(3). The mission of CLRS, emanating from Seattle's Rainier Valley, is to convene, support and advance community-based initiatives that promote justice, fairness, the collaboration of cultures and the social well-being of our children and all of its citizens and surrounding neighborhoods in King County and the world. The CLRS has currently chosen Urban Impact as its fiscal sponsor to process all donations for the 180 Program.
